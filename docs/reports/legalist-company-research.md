## Findings

### 1. Company overview and what Legalist does
- Legalist currently describes itself as a "technology-powered investment firm" and an "institutional alternative asset management firm" rather than as a software vendor or traditional law firm. On its homepage and About page, it says it specializes in alternative assets and specifically focuses on legal assets.[1][2]
- The company says it was founded in 2016, incubated at Y Combinator, and launched its first fund in 2017.[1]
- Legalist states that it combines proprietary sourcing technology with rigorous legal underwriting to make investments and generate risk-adjusted returns for clients.[2]
- Legalist says it manages more than $1.5 billion; its About page shows an AUM progression from $10M in 2017 to $1.6B in 2024/current, indicating significant scale-up over time.[1][2]

### 2. Product and service positioning
- Legalist positions itself as an asset manager powered by proprietary technology rather than a pure software company. The core promise is to use data-driven sourcing and underwriting to invest in legal and regulatory-risk-heavy assets at scale.[1][2][6]
- The company’s public application flow suggests three current financing/product lines: Litigation Financing, Bankruptcy, and Government Contractor Financing.[7]
- Public company materials and curated press emphasize legal assets and related credit strategies. Legalist’s own news page highlights AI origination technology and a flagship government receivables strategy, suggesting the company wants to be seen as a tech-enabled specialist across adjacent opportunistic credit categories, not only classic litigation finance.[3][4]

### 3. Target customers and use cases
- On the capital-raising side, Legalist says it manages money for endowments, foundations, hospitals, insurance companies, and family offices, indicating an institutional LP base rather than retail investors.[1]
- On the asset-origination side, the Apply page indicates prospective borrowers/claimants/customers include parties seeking litigation financing, bankruptcy financing, or government contractor financing.[7]
- Its stated focus on legal assets and government receivables implies use cases where outcomes depend on legal process, claims enforcement, bankruptcy proceedings, or government payment timing; this aligns with third-party descriptions of Legalist as investing in alternative assets involving legal and regulatory risk.[2][5]
